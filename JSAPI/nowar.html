War does not lead to peace

Now that the war in Iraq has started, there is all the more reason to speak out against the war. We respect the potential sacrifices soldiers make in going into battle, but believe that there is still no just cause for going to war in the first place. I have included below several of today's Seattle Post-Intelligencer's letters to the editor. They talk of the huge global benefits if only a fraction of the money to be spent on war were used to eliminate the basic humanitarian problems of the world, the lack of just cause, the dire state of the US economy, and the huge problem of rebuilding Iraq that Bush minimizes or ignores.

There are many excellent reasons for working to find a peaceful solution to the Iraq crisis - a crisis which has been created by the US, which has no connection to the terrorist attacks of September 11th, and which has turned world sympathy for the US into a growing distrust and hatred of the American people.

While there is no doubt that Saddam is a tyrant, there is no reason to put the fate of the world in the hands of a bully. Bush has precious little concern for the livelihood of the majority of people living in the US, has no appreciation or respect for the global political or natural environment (as well as the natural environment of the US), and no real respect for the lives of Iraqi civilians. He became the president of the US by being a bully, and he threatens to disregard the wishes of the UN while bribing UN members over to his side. If he flaunts the UN (especially after breaking so many important international treaties - global warming etc) then he has no justification for attacking a country on the grounds that it is breaking UN orders.

Despite what he says, he does not have the best interests of the Iraqi people at heart, who are terrified at having bombs fall on them (after all, it is estimated that 10,000 Iraqi civilians will die in an attack which will create 1,000,000 refugees). He cannot be allowed to split human beings into US citizens and the rest of the world when it comes to the value of life. Nor does he seem to be overly concerned for the people in America likely to be the brunt of increased terrorist attacks if he starts a war in Iraq. 

Seattle PI, Letters to the Editor, Thursday, March 20, 2003

Peace rhetoric shouldn't stop when battle begins 

With war upon us, there will now be calls for those who have opposed this tragedy to silence ourselves. It's as if, having struggled to stave off an encroaching fire, one is supposed to look on approvingly as the house goes up in flames. 

When my government proposes actions I consider harmful to the interests of this nation and to the world, I consider it my duty to oppose them. That responsibility does not diminish when the government actually takes such actions.

Even as acts of war go, bombing cities is barbaric. Going to war means our hands are caked with blood. For that reason alone, the standard for doing so is very high. It is not met in the present instance and commencing hostilities does not change that.

This administration has deliberately misled Americans about this war, and I am not about to support the man who holds the office of president merely because he happens to occupy it and wields his power with a recklessness unprecedented in U.S. history. How can one "support our troops" by keeping silent when the president risks their lives unnecessarily?

President Bush assumes that in resorting to military force he will have the unquestioning support of Americans, no matter how disastrous the consequences. To silence ourselves now would be to announce to this administration, and to those that follow, that we will never hold them to account. What could be more irresponsible?

Jon Reinsch
Seattle

When troops are sent, the cause should be just 

When a man goes through contortions to justify his actions, something is usually amiss. Take President Bush's St. Patrick's Day ultimatum, along with Colin Powell's news conference of the same day: Both men put a lot of weight on United Nations Resolution 1441 to authorize a U.S. invasion, and yet the same United Nations is withholding support for the invasion. Also by trying to bribe Security Council members, offering them new foreign aid packages contingent on support for a second resolution (only to lose anyway), the administration has disgraced the country in the eyes of the world. 

I'm no enthusiast for the United Nations or for world government in any form, but by going to the United Nations, in effect the United States submitted the matter to arbitration. When the arbitrator rules in an unexpected or undesired way, a party to the arbitration is not justified in walking out and treating the issue as if the arbitrator's view doesn't matter. 

We can support U.S. troops by bringing them home from the Middle East. We can support U.S. troops by dissuading their commander in chief from committing them to a grand crime. We can support our Catholic troops by allowing them to live in accord with just-war doctrine. 

Yes, there is such a thing as just war. But, among other religious leaders, the pope is convinced a U.S. invasion of Iraq is not justifiable. We owe our troops a just cause. 

Bob Struble
Bremerton

Promised tax cuts are needed elsewhere 

With 9/11, the flags came out and patriotism seemed to be measured by how large the flag or how many flags were flying. Then came the downturn in the economy and now the war with Iraq. To me, the sign of true patriotism is the willingness of its citizens to make sacrifices to help keep the country afloat. The "no more taxes" campaign should have been muted by 9/11 and its aftermath. I am amazed that the Bush administration and the Republican Party do not consider the war on terrorism, the faltering economy and now the Iraqi war as significantly valid reasons to reverse the decisions relating to tax cuts made prior to these events.

It is extremely important for U.S. patriots to contact their congressional representatives now and let them know that they are willing to forgo a tax cut to help support this country through these trying times. Those of us who love this country hate to see it going through such turmoil. We need to continue to support the federal government financially so that it can begin to help improve the economy, get the unemployed back to work, pay for the war on terrorism and now the new war with Iraq. 

For many of us, taxation is our only opportunity to serve our country. It is through taxation that this country has maintained its prosperity and effectiveness. All its patriots, individuals and corporations, should be willing to pay their fair share to make this country great again.

Kathy Harris
Seattle

Bush wants to pull strings of a puppet 

I have read the U.N. resolutions regarding the government of Iraq and agree that the use of force to disarm it is authorized without further action by the Security Council. 

This, however, does not mean I agree with the tyrannical and despotic behavior of our current administration and, specifically, President Bush.

Bush and his administration seem to have their own agenda, as though this conflict were pre-determined. I don't believe this conflict is about disarmament. I believe Resolution 1441 is simply a means for the president to remove a dictator and replace him with a puppet government, no matter what the cost. 

The United States is supposed to be the world leader in democracy, but our government has not acted according to the will of the people. The American public has been fed lies, false accusations and rhetoric so confounding that the average citizen doesn't know what to believe. 

Public opinion polls now show support for this invasion, but are they accurate? I believe Americans have simply accepted a war they had no say in, and now support our troops in their endeavors.

In the past, the United States has been respected by countries around the world as a fair and just nation. A nation to be sought out in times of humane rights violations, oppression, famine and disaster, a nation of high morality and standards of conduct. This world opinion is part of what makes me proud to be an American. However, my pride has somewhat diminished over recent events. 

World opinion is now against us. We have become the dictator, the warmonger, the tyrant and the despot.

The media are saturated with politicians and news personalities throwing the blame at one another. It's the Democrats, it's the Republicans, it's France, it's Sen. This or Sen. That. So and so supported this back then, etc.

Let's stop the bull and put the blame where it should be -- on President Bush. He made the choice to invade. He defied world opinion. He went against the wishes of our allies. He angered the world populations. He put our citizens at a heightened risk of terrorist attack. He put our military in harm's way. He put thousands of innocent civilians around the world in harm's way.

Fixing global problems would cost less than war 

I admit there are arguments on both sides of the issue about war against Iraq. When people consider the question, however, I hope they will keep the following in mind. It has certainly helped me make up my mind.

The United Nations has estimated that it would take about $40 billion to address all basic unmet human needs in the world; that is, hunger, basic medical care, basic sanitation and minimal education. This number comes from UNICEF. Although this seems like a very low number, the reality is that micro loans and vaccination programs go an incredible distance on a little money compared with the $1 million cost of one smart bomb and the incredible cost of equipping and maintaining an divisionary force in Iraq. Forty billion is still a heck of a lot of money. But for the Iraq war and the rebuilding of Iraq after the war, estimates range from $400 billion to $600 billion from the U.S. Treasury for the total bill.

So what if we call the U.N. basic-needs figures low, and multiply them fivefold in order to be sure we had enough to do the job. Call it $200 billion. Then, what if instead of spending $400 billion to $600 billion to invade Iraq and to rebuild the country after we destroy much of its infrastructure, we instead decided to simply let the inspections process continue. Iraq undoubtedly would be contained, we would avoid the wrath of world opinion now marshaled against us, and we would avoid violating the U.N. Charter by launching an invasion into the territory of another member state without the authorization of the Security Council. These are all good things.

Then suppose we spent $200 billion toward unmet human needs around the would, again five times what the United Nations says it would take, but still saving $200 billion to $400 billion compared with an invasion of Iraq. Can anyone seriously argue that we would not be more secure having eradicated hunger and other unmet basic human needs around the world than we would be as the result of an invasion of Iraq? We could do what humankind has long dreamed of doing, ending unmet basic human needs. One has to ask: Isn't this more important than invading Iraq? And we could do it for less than half the cost. Viewed this way, deciding to invade Iraq instead of ending unmet human needs is an almost incomprehensible decision.

I wonder why is it that we are able to generate the domestic political will to spend $400 billion to $600 billion on a war that is plainly motivated by anger and fear, but we are unable to generate the domestic political will to spend $200 billion, or even $40 billion, to end all unmet basic human needs worldwide, an act that would be principally motivated by empathy and love for our fellow human beings. It is odd to me that more people are not discussing this rather stark dichotomy, including those in the mainstream media. All this leaves me wondering what our choices say about who we really are as a human species, peaceful or warlike. The answer, based upon our actions, seems pretty clear.

David B. Richardson
Seattle
